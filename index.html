Final Portfolio Case Study

Making a biomedical innovation course more learner-facing, action-oriented, and coherent in Canvas.

Project Overview

What I changed, and why it mattered

I did not completely redesign the course structure. The weekly modules were already clearly organized. Instead, I focused on improving how learners moved through the existing pages. In several places, the original course provided strong resources and relevant content, but the pages still felt more informational than instructional. My revisions aimed to make those pages more learner-facing, more action-oriented, and more closely tied to concrete next steps.

Original course strengths

  • Clear weekly module sequence
  • Strong emphasis on customer discovery and business case development
  • Useful resource links, worksheets, and videos by track
  • Logical progression across innovation, regulation, and commercialization

My redesign focus

  • Clarify why each weekly topic matters at that point in the course
  • Guide learners' attention before they watch videos or use resources
  • Add lightweight activities and prompts that support project-based application
  • Strengthen the connection between page content and concrete deliverables
Course-wide Design Pattern

A clearer rhythm across the course pages

Across the revisions, I tried to establish a consistent design logic. Instead of treating each page mainly as a place to hold information, I used a repeating learner-support pattern that could work across different topics and weeks.

1
Explain why the topic matters now

Start by situating the page in the learner’s current stage of project development.

2
Focus attention with guiding questions

Help students know what to look for before they read, watch, or download anything.

3
Add a lightweight way to apply the idea

Use short prompts or activities so students can connect the topic back to their own innovation.

4
Connect the page to a deliverable or next step

Make the learning path feel more coherent instead of leaving the transition to the next task implicit.

Implemented Canvas Revisions

Selected pages I revised directly in Canvas

The sections below follow the order of the course itself. Together, they show how I revised page framing, resource use, learner prompts, and assignment alignment while keeping the course’s existing structure recognizable.

Pre-course addition

Device Concept Brief: from broad value proposition to clearer innovation framing

In the pre-course customer discovery section, the original materials already introduced stakeholder mapping, value proposition work, and customer discovery interviews. What still felt missing was a structured step that helps learners turn that early discovery work into a clearer device or innovation concept.

  • I drafted a new page called Device Concept Brief: From Value Proposition to Device Framing.
  • This addition helps students connect unmet need, intended use, target user, use setting, and early value proposition before moving into later work on regulation, IP, and commercialization.
  • The page functions as a bridge from broad customer discovery language into more focused medical-device framing.

New learner-facing prompt page designed to help students clarify the device concept before later commercialization work.

Week 1 Opportunity sizing and monetization

A more guided regulatory starting point for early project reasoning

Week 1 originally introduced opportunity sizing and monetization through useful resources, but the page still felt somewhat like a resource page rather than a learner-guided page. I revised it to create a clearer entry point into early reasoning for medical devices.

  • I rewrote the opening to explain why early regulatory awareness matters before thinking about market size, monetization, or adoption.
  • I added a lightweight class overview and framing language so learners can begin thinking in terms of lower, moderate, or higher risk.
  • I also designed related Week 1 activities such as Who Pays vs. Who Benefits? and Choose Your Route to help teams distinguish users, beneficiaries, decision-makers, and payers.

Revised Week 1 page introducing an early regulatory starting point before opportunity sizing and monetization decisions.

Week 1 assignment alignment

Making the submission page reflect the revised learning goals

I also revised the Week 1 submission page so that the assignment instructions aligned more directly with the new Week 1 content. Rather than leaving the connection between page reading and submission implicit, the updated prompts ask teams to carry their reasoning into a short written deliverable.

  • Students are prompted to explain the opportunity, identify who is affected, distinguish who benefits and who pays, and select one plausible route.
  • This turns Week 1 into a more coherent instructional sequence rather than a page plus an only loosely related submission point.
  • The revision supports a stronger transition from learning content to visible student reasoning.

Aligned submission prompts that ask students to turn Week 1 reasoning into a concise written response.

Week 3 Regulatory considerations

Turning a resource repository into a guided regulatory learning page

Week 3 was one of the most important revisions. The original page already contained track-specific worksheets, videos, IND versus IDE resources, and consultation links, but the page still felt more like a collection of materials than a structured learning experience.

  • I added a short introduction explaining why regulatory considerations matter early, not only at the end.
  • I created a How to use this page section so students know what to review first, what to download, and how to use the track-specific materials.
  • I clarified that the goal is not immediate certainty, but early regulatory questioning about evidence, documentation, and pathway uncertainty.

Reframed Week 3 page with clearer purpose, clearer entry points, and more intentional use of track-specific resources.

Week 4 Making your business case

Rewriting a weekly announcement into a real learning guide

The original Week 4 page explained that students would focus on customer discovery before the finale. That message was useful, but I wanted the page to do more than function as an announcement. My revision turned it into a weekly guide that supports deeper preparation for the final business case.

  • I rewrote the page to explain why this week matters and how customer discovery strengthens the final case.
  • I added guiding questions about what the team still needs to learn, which stakeholder perspectives are missing, and what evidence could strengthen the pitch.
  • I reframed office hours as a meaningful design and feedback opportunity instead of a simple reminder.

Week 4 redesigned as a weekly guide, with focus questions and a stronger link between customer discovery and final pitch preparation.

Additional Design Extensions

Further revisions integrated into the broader course redesign

In addition to the pages shown above, I also proposed or drafted several smaller activity-based revisions that strengthen the course without changing its overall structure.

Week 2

Competitive analysis and IP activities

I designed activity ideas such as Better Than What? and Search Like a Detective to give students a lighter, more concrete entry point into competitive analysis and prior art thinking before the formal assignment.

Week 3 checkpoint

Presentation guide revision

I revised the Week 3 progress presentation instructions so the page communicates not only what slides students should include, but also what kind of performance the checkpoint presentation is meant to support.

Week 5

Milestone interpretation and reflection prompts

For the Critical Milestones page, I added interpretation prompts and reflection questions so students can connect milestone videos back to their own innovation rather than simply selecting and watching a track-specific resource.

Competency Growth

How this project strengthened my design abilities

Beyond the page-level revisions, this portfolio also reflects how my thinking developed during the internship. What began as course review gradually became a more intentional LXD process: identifying friction points, clarifying learning purpose, writing for action, and building stronger alignment between page content and learner performance.

Growth area 01

Moving from review comments to instructional decisions

Earlier in the project, I was mainly identifying what felt unclear or missing. Through this work, I became better at turning those observations into design decisions: what explanation should come first, what guiding question should be added, what kind of prompt would help learners act, and what next step the page should support.

Growth area 02

Designing with learner action in mind

One of the biggest shifts for me was learning to see course pages not simply as places that hold content, but as moments in a learner journey. That changed how I revised pages. Instead of only making them clearer, I tried to make them usable, so that students would know what to notice, what to reflect on, and what to do next.

Growth area 03

Working inside a specialized domain without losing learner clarity

This project also strengthened my confidence in translating expert-facing content for learners. Biomedical innovation, regulation, and commercialization involve complex concepts, but this experience helped me practice preserving that complexity while still building clearer entry points into the material.

Growth area 04

Using consistency as a design strategy

Across the revised pages, I worked toward a repeatable pattern: explain why the topic matters, focus attention, support lightweight application, and connect the page to a deliverable or next step. Developing and applying that pattern helped me think more like a learning designer working across a system rather than editing pages in isolation.

What I can now do more confidently

Skills this project made visible

Analyze an existing course with an LXD lens

I can now identify where a course is strong, where learners may still get stuck, and where the most useful revisions are likely to have the biggest effect.

Write more learner-facing Canvas instruction

I became more intentional about how page introductions, guiding questions, and assignment language can shape learner attention and make expectations more actionable.

Connect page design to broader course coherence

This project helped me see that consistency in weekly page logic is itself a form of instructional support, especially in project-based and professional learning contexts.

Final Reflection

From informational pages to learning supports

Across all of these changes, my main design goal was consistency and usability. I wanted the course pages to follow a clearer pattern: first explain why the topic matters, then focus students’ attention with a few guiding questions, then give them a lightweight way to apply the idea to their own project, and finally connect that work to a concrete deliverable or next step. Rather than treating pages mainly as places to hold information, I tried to make them work more like learning supports. This portfolio represents that shift clearly: from reviewing course materials to actively shaping a more guided, coherent, and actionable learner experience in Canvas.
